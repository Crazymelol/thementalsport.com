# Copywriting

## Overview

Good copy is about the reader's desired outcome, not the product's features. It's clear before it's clever. Write to one person, lead with the benefit they care about, and remove every word that doesn't earn its place.

**Core principle:** Sell the transformation, not the tool. The reader doesn't want a drill; they want a hole — actually, they want the shelf up and their partner impressed.

## The Order of Operations

1. **Know the one reader** — their current pain, desired state, and the objection blocking them.
2. **Lead with the outcome** — the headline promises the after-state, not the mechanism.
3. **Make it specific** — numbers, concrete nouns, real scenarios. Specificity = credibility.
4. **Handle the objection** — name the doubt and answer it (proof, guarantee, social proof).
5. **One clear CTA** — tell them exactly what to do next, framed as the next step toward the outcome.

## Headline Patterns That Work

- Promise + specificity: "Calm your race-day nerves in 6 minutes a day."
- Problem → relief: "Choking under pressure? Train the mental side too."
- Avoid: vague hype ("The best app ever"), feature-first ("Now with AI"), me-focused ("We're excited to announce").

## Voice & Mechanics

- Second person ("you"), active voice, short sentences. Read it aloud — if you stumble, cut.
- Concrete > abstract. "Sleeps 4, packs in 90 seconds" beats "versatile and convenient."
- Cut: "very", "really", "in order to", "we are committed to", throat-clearing intros.
- One idea per sentence. White space is a feature.

## Match the Channel & Brand Voice

Ad ≠ landing page ≠ product description. If a `brand-voice` guide exists, copy must match its tone, vocabulary, and forbidden words. Persuasion never overrides brand consistency.

## Common Mistakes

- Feature lists with no "so what" (translate every feature to a benefit).
- Writing to "everyone" → resonates with no one.
- Clever wordplay that obscures the offer.
- Burying or weakening the CTA ("learn more" instead of "start your free trial").
- Adjective inflation instead of concrete proof.
