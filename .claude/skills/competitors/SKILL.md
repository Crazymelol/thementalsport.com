# Competitive Analysis

## Overview
The goal is not to copy competitors but to find the gap they leave open and position against it. Study them to understand the market and the customer, then differentiate deliberately rather than imitate.

## Teardown Framework
1. **Identify**: direct (same solution), indirect (different solution, same job), and the status quo ("do nothing"). The status quo is often the real competitor.
2. **Funnel walk**: experience their funnel as a customer — ads, landing page, signup, onboarding, pricing page, emails, checkout. Note hooks, friction, and proof.
3. **Positioning**: who they target, the core promise, the category they claim, price tier.
4. **Offer & pricing**: tiers, value metric, guarantees, anchors.
5. **Proof & moat**: reviews, social proof, brand, distribution, switching costs.
6. **Voice of their customers**: read their reviews — praise reveals what to match, complaints reveal your opening.

## Quick Reference
| Dimension | Capture | Use it to |
|---|---|---|
| Positioning | Promise, target, category | Find an unclaimed angle |
| Pricing | Tiers, value metric | Price gap / better packaging |
| Acquisition | Channels, ad angles | Where to compete or avoid |
| Reviews | Top complaints/praise | Differentiation + messaging |
| Gaps | Unserved segment/need | Your wedge |

**Positioning map:** plot competitors on two axes that matter to buyers (e.g. price vs simplicity); aim for an empty, valuable quadrant.

## Common Mistakes
- Feature-copying instead of finding a differentiated position — leads to "us too" parity.
- Ignoring the status-quo / "do nothing" competitor.
- Analyzing only direct rivals and missing indirect substitutes.
- Treating competitor tactics as proof they work (they may be failing too).
- One-time snapshot instead of ongoing monitoring.
- Mining features but skipping their customer reviews — the richest signal.
- Racing to match on price instead of competing on a different value axis.
