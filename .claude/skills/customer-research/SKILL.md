# Customer Research

## Overview
Great messaging is discovered, not invented — it comes from the customer's own words about the job they're hiring you to do. Research the struggle and the desired outcome, then mirror that language back.

## Methods
1. **Voice-of-customer mining** (fastest): pull reviews (yours + competitors'), support tickets, sales-call notes, Reddit/forums, search queries. Tag exact phrases for pains, desires, objections, and triggers.
2. **JTBD interviews**: interview recent buyers about the timeline — what happened, the trigger ("first thought"), what they tried, what pushed them to switch, the outcome they wanted. Focus on the job, not the product.
3. **Surveys**: validate at scale after qualitative work. Ask the Sean Ellis question ("how disappointed if you couldn't use this?") and open-ended "what nearly stopped you from buying?"
4. **Synthesize**: cluster into pains, gains, jobs, objections, switching triggers. Quote verbatim.

## Quick Reference
| Method | Best for | Output |
|---|---|---|
| Review/ticket mining | Fast VoC language | Phrases, objections |
| JTBD interviews | Why they switched | Triggers, struggle, outcome |
| Surveys | Validation at scale | Priorities, segments |
| Sales/support listening | Live objections | Friction points |

**Good interview questions:** "Walk me through the day you decided to look for a solution." "What were you using before and what frustrated you?" "What almost stopped you from buying?" "What does success look like?"

## Common Mistakes
- Leading questions or pitching the product instead of listening.
- Asking what people *want* (speculative) vs what they actually *did* (behavioral).
- Relying only on surveys without qualitative depth first.
- Paraphrasing answers into corporate-speak, losing the verbatim language that converts.
- Interviewing the wrong people (prospects who never bought, or non-users).
- Stopping after a few chats — research until patterns repeat and saturate.
- Collecting insights but never feeding them into copy, offers, and positioning.
