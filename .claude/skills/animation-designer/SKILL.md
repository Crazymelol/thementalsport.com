# Animation & Motion Design

## Overview
Motion directs attention and conveys relationships. Good animation feels physical (eased, weighted), serves meaning, and gets out of the way. If motion doesn't guide the eye or explain something, cut it.

## Framework
1. **Purpose first**: Does this motion direct attention, show cause/effect, or just decorate? Decoration is a liability.
2. **Easing**: Almost never linear. Use ease-out for entrances (fast in, settle), ease-in for exits, ease-in-out for moves. Add slight overshoot for life.
3. **Timing**: Short and snappy. UI/text 150-400ms; scene transitions 300-800ms. Stagger related elements 30-80ms apart.
4. **Principles** (Disney 12): anticipation, follow-through, overlap, squash/stretch, secondary action add weight and believability.
5. **Hierarchy**: One primary motion at a time. Don't animate everything at once.

## When motion helps vs distracts
- **Helps**: revealing structure, guiding to a CTA, showing change over time, softening hard cuts.
- **Distracts**: gratuitous spins/bounces, slow fades that stall pace, motion competing with the message, looping idle animations during reading.

## Quick Reference
| Use | Easing | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Text/element in | ease-out | 200-350ms |
| Element out | ease-in | 150-250ms |
| Move/reposition | ease-in-out | 300-500ms |
| Emphasis pop | overshoot/spring | 250-400ms |
| Scene transition | ease-in-out | 400-800ms |
| Stagger offset | — | 30-80ms |

## Common Mistakes
- Linear easing everywhere (feels robotic).
- Durations too long; motion that makes the viewer wait.
- Animating too many things simultaneously; no focal point.
- No anticipation or follow-through; motion starts/stops dead.
- Decorative motion that competes with text the viewer is reading.
- Inconsistent easing/timing across a piece.
