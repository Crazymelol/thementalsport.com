# Marketing Psychology

## Overview
People decide emotionally and justify rationally. Cialdini's seven principles are reliable levers for influencing behavior — use them to remove hesitation and add a nudge, never to manipulate against the buyer's interest.

## The Seven Principles
| Principle | What it does | Tactics |
|---|---|---|
| Reciprocity | Free value creates obligation to give back | Lead magnets, free trials, useful content, surprise bonuses |
| Scarcity | Limited = more valuable; loss aversion | Real deadlines, limited stock/seats, "only X left" |
| Social proof | People copy others, esp. similar peers | Reviews, counts ("12,000 athletes"), testimonials, UGC, logos |
| Authority | Trust credible experts/signals | Credentials, data, press, expert endorsement |
| Commitment/consistency | Small yes leads to bigger yes | Micro-commitments, multi-step opt-ins, free→paid ladder |
| Liking | We say yes to people we like/relate to | Founder story, shared values, relatable UGC, warmth |
| Unity | Shared identity ("one of us") | "For [tribe]", community, in-group language |

## Application Order
1. Identify the single biggest objection blocking the action.
2. Match the principle that dissolves it (risk → reciprocity/guarantee; doubt → social proof/authority; delay → scarcity).
3. Place it at the decision point (near CTA, price, checkout).
4. Keep claims true — fake scarcity or fake reviews destroy trust on discovery.

## Common Mistakes
- Fake or manufactured scarcity/countdowns that reset — kills credibility fast.
- Generic testimonials ("Great product!") instead of specific, results-based proof from similar customers.
- Stacking every principle at once, creating a loud, untrustworthy page.
- Borrowing authority you can't back up.
- Using persuasion to push a bad-fit purchase — drives refunds and churn.
- Ignoring loss aversion: framing gains when "don't lose X" is stronger.
- Placing proof far from the decision instead of beside the CTA/price.
