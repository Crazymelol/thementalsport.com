# Lead Magnets

## Overview
A great lead magnet solves one specific problem fast and naturally leads to the paid offer — narrow and immediately useful beats comprehensive and overwhelming.

## Design Principles
1. **Hyper-specific** — one painful problem for one audience, not a broad guide.
2. **Fast value** — consumable and useful in minutes; quick win builds trust.
3. **Bridge to the offer** — the gap it leaves is what the paid product fills.
4. **High perceived value** — feels worth paying for, given free.
5. **Easy to consume** — checklist, template, swipe file > 80-page ebook.

## Choosing Format
- **Checklist/cheat sheet** — fast, actionable, low effort.
- **Template/swipe file** — done-for-you, high value.
- **Mini-course/email series** — also nurtures via inbox.
- **Quiz/calculator** — interactive, segments leads.
- **Toolkit/resource list** — curated shortcut.

## Alignment Test
Ask: does using this magnet make the paid offer the obvious next step? If the freebie satisfies completely with no natural next need, it's misaligned.

## Quick Reference
| Format | Effort | Convert speed | Best for |
|--------|--------|---------------|----------|
| Checklist | Low | Fast | Top-funnel |
| Template | Med | Fast | Direct-to-offer |
| Mini-course | High | Medium | Nurture |
| Quiz | Med | Fast | Segmentation |

## Common Mistakes
- Too broad — "ultimate guide to everything" delivers no quick win.
- High friction to consume (long PDFs nobody finishes).
- No connection to the paid offer (attracts wrong leads).
- Low perceived value — generic, obviously AI-spun filler.
- Promising more than the form/offer can deliver.
- Optimizing volume of opt-ins over quality/fit.
