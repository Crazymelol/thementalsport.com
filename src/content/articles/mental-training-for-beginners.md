Most athletes spend zero hours per week on deliberate mental performance training.

They think about the mental game — worry about it, wish they were better at it — but they don't *train* it. Not because they don't believe it matters. Because nobody showed them how.

This is that guide.

## The Five Foundational Mental Skills

### 1. Goal Setting

Mental training starts with clarity about what you're trying to achieve and why.

Most athletes have vague intentions ("I want to get better," "I want to perform well"). These don't function as training targets because they're unmeasurable and don't drive specific behavior.

**The three-tier framework:**

*Outcome goals*: What you want to achieve (win the state championship, qualify for nationals, make the team)

*Performance goals*: Measurable standards that would achieve the outcome (finish in top 3 at regionals, shoot 85% from the free throw line, complete 20 pull-ups)

*Process goals*: Daily behaviors that produce performance results (visualization practice every morning, pre-competition routine consistency, film study every Tuesday)

The critical insight: process goals are the only category fully in your control. Outcome and performance goals depend on conditions, opponents, and circumstances you can't control. Process goals depend only on your choices.

**Build it**: Write three goals at each level right now. Review weekly. Adjust process goals when performance goals suggest something isn't working.

### 2. Self-Talk Management

Your internal monologue runs continuously during training and competition. Left unmanaged, it defaults to whatever habitual patterns you've developed — which for most athletes includes significant self-critical, catastrophic, or distracted thought.

Managed deliberately, it becomes a performance tool.

**Types of performance self-talk:**

*Instructional*: Technical cues that direct specific behaviors ("drive through the wall," "rotate the hips," "stay tall")

*Motivational*: Statements that sustain effort ("keep going," "you've prepared for this," "one more rep")

*Reframing*: Thoughts that interrupt unhelpful patterns and replace them with useful ones ("that was a mistake — process cue — next play")

**Build it**: Write your competition self-talk script this week. Include: one instructional cue per skill you're executing, one motivational phrase for high-effort moments, and one reset phrase for after mistakes. Practice these in training until they're automatic.

### 3. Concentration and Attention Control

The ability to direct attention where you want it, for as long as you want it there, and to return it quickly when it wanders — this is the concentration skill.

It's trainable through mindfulness meditation, and it's the foundational skill that everything else in mental performance rests on.

**Build it**: Start with 10 minutes of breath-focused meditation daily. Sit quietly, focus on the sensation of breathing, and return attention every time it wanders. This is the practice. Do it every day. Results emerge in weeks, not days.

### 4. Anxiety Management

Pre-competition anxiety is near-universal in competitive athletes. The goal isn't to eliminate it — anxiety in appropriate doses is performance-enhancing. The goal is to manage it so it doesn't exceed your optimal zone.

**The core toolkit:**

*Breathing*: The physiological sigh (double inhale, long exhale) is the fastest evidence-based anxiety reducer. Learn it. Practice it daily. Use it in competition.

*Cognitive reappraisal*: "I'm not nervous, I'm ready" — reinterpret the physical anxiety state as preparation arousal rather than threat response.

*Pre-performance routine*: Consistent routine creates psychological predictability that reduces anxiety by making the unknown familiar.

**Build it**: Identify your specific anxiety symptoms (physical, cognitive, behavioral). Choose one technique from the toolkit for each. Practice each technique during training sessions before applying in competition.

### 5. Imagery and Visualization

Mental rehearsal — vivid, detailed imagination of performance — activates the same neural pathways as physical execution. Done correctly, visualization is practice.

Done poorly (vague, inconsistent, outcome-focused), it's nothing.

**The PETTLEP framework for effective visualization:**

- **Physical**: physically simulate the performance position during visualization
- **Environment**: visualize the actual competition environment as specifically as possible
- **Task**: visualize the specific task (not just "performing well")
- **Timing**: use real-time timing, not sped-up imagery
- **Learning**: update imagery as skills develop
- **Emotion**: include the emotional experience of competition
- **Perspective**: use first-person (internal) perspective for skill rehearsal

**Build it**: 10 minutes of visualization practice 3-4 times per week. Start with your best recent performance. Then visualize an upcoming competition: the environment, your warmup, key moments, adversity scenarios, and your responses.

## Your First Week

**Monday**: Write your goals (3 levels). Write your self-talk script.

**Tuesday**: 10 minutes of breath meditation. Your first visualization session (best recent performance, 10 minutes).

**Wednesday**: 10 minutes of breath meditation. Practice your self-talk script during training.

**Thursday**: 10 minutes of breath meditation. Visualization session (upcoming competition, 10 minutes).

**Friday**: 10 minutes of breath meditation. Anxiety management practice (physiological sigh × 10).

**Weekend**: Rest or continue meditation. Journal for 5 minutes: what did you notice this week?

## The Most Important Thing

Consistency over intensity.

Ten minutes of meditation every day for a month produces more lasting adaptation than three 30-minute sessions per week. Mental training is exactly like physical training in this respect: frequency and consistency build the neural adaptations; sporadic intensity does not.

Start small. Start now. Don't stop.

The mental game is a skill. You just started training it.
