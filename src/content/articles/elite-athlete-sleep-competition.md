The night before a competition, most athletes make the same mistakes:

- Lie in bed trying desperately to sleep
- Check their phone obsessively
- Watch their opponent's highlight reel
- Change their bedtime to "get more rest"
- Mentally replay every possible worst-case scenario

All of these are wrong. And the sleep science is unambiguous about why.

## The Brutal Truth About Pre-Competition Sleep

Here's the first thing sports sleep scientists will tell you: **the night before competition is the least important night's sleep of the week.**

Your performance tomorrow is primarily determined by the quality of your sleep over the past week — specifically nights two and three before competition. One bad night before an event has relatively minor performance consequences if the preceding nights were quality.

This matters because most athletes enter competition eve with catastrophic anxiety about sleeping — which makes them sleep worse — which they then blame for the poor performance that actually resulted from other factors.

The best thing you can do for competition-eve sleep? Stop caring so much about it.

## What Actually Disrupts Pre-Competition Sleep

### Cortisol from novelty and anxiety

The brain releases cortisol in response to perceived threats — including competitive events. Cortisol is a wakefulness hormone. The more anxious you are about competing and sleeping, the more cortisol you produce, the worse you sleep.

The intervention: Reduce the novelty. Same routine, same environment, same bedtime, same meal. Novelty = threat signal. Routine = safety signal.

### Blue light and stimulation

Screens suppress melatonin (the sleep-onset hormone) for 90+ minutes after exposure. Most athletes spend competition eve doom-scrolling. This is physiologically guaranteed to make sleep onset worse.

**Rule**: Screens off 90-120 minutes before target sleep time.

### Alcohol as "relaxation"

Some athletes use alcohol to manage pre-competition anxiety. Alcohol impairs REM sleep — the cognitively restorative phase. You fall asleep faster but wake unrefreshed and with degraded cognitive performance.

Never before competition.

### Excessive caffeine timing

Caffeine's half-life is 5-7 hours. A coffee at 3pm is still 50% active at 8pm. Competition day is not the time to experiment with caffeine timing. Your caffeine protocol should be identical to your training day protocol.

## What Elite Athletes Actually Do

### LeBron James

James has made his sleep practice famous: 8-10 hours per night, with naps. He employs a sleep coach, tracks HRV and sleep stages, and treats sleep preparation with the same intentionality as training.

His pre-competition routine: consistent bedtime, blackout curtains, cool room temperature (65-68°F), no screens after a set time.

### Roger Federer

Federer famously sleeps 10-12 hours per night during tournament weeks. He extends sleep opportunity during competition periods and uses napping strategically. His pre-match sleep routine is identical regardless of round or opponent.

### Usain Bolt

The fastest man in history credited his sleep practice as a critical component of his dominance. In his autobiography, Bolt describes sleeping 8-10 hours before major competitions and treating the pre-race night as completely routine — no special preparations.

## The Pre-Competition Sleep Protocol

**One week out**: Extend sleep opportunity by 30-60 minutes per night. You can build sleep reserve in advance.

**Three nights out**: This is actually the most important night. Prioritize it absolutely.

**Two nights out**: Second most important. Same treatment.

**Competition eve**:
- Same bedtime as always (±30 minutes)
- Same pre-sleep routine (shower, light reading, journaling — whatever your established routine is)
- Room temperature 65-68°F (the optimal sleep temperature range)
- Complete darkness (blackout curtains or sleep mask)
- No screens 90 minutes before bed
- No performance analysis, opponent research, or competition-related media
- If sleep doesn't come within 30-40 minutes: get up, do something calming in dim light for 20 minutes, return to bed

**If you wake during the night**: Normal. Don't check the time. Don't start thinking about competition. Use a simple breath focus (count exhales from 10 down) to return to drowsiness.

## The Athlete Who Doesn't Sleep

Some athletes genuinely cannot sleep the night before major competitions.

Adrenaline, cortisol, and psychological arousal override the sleep drive. This is normal and not catastrophic.

Research shows that one night of significant sleep deprivation (even total sleep deprivation) has minimal effects on strength, speed, and power output. It does impair mood and perceived exertion — but performance itself is more resilient to one bad night than most athletes believe.

If you know you won't sleep well the night before:
1. Stop fighting it — acceptance reduces the cortisol
2. Lie still in the dark — physical rest has recovery value even without sleep
3. Trust that the preceding nights' sleep has loaded your body for performance
4. Use the wakefulness productively: visualization, controlled breathing, mental rehearsal

The night before a competition is not where your performance is built. That work was done in training.

Trust it.
