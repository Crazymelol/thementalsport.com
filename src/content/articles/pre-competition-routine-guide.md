Most athletes prepare their body. Almost none prepare their mind.

The week before competition, a typical athlete trains less, eats better, and sleeps more. Good. But they also replay worst-case scenarios, over-analyze their opponent, break their established sleep schedule "to get extra rest," and arrive at competition day with a mind that's never been prepared for this moment.

The pre-competition routine fixes this.

## Why Routines Work

Routines create psychological predictability. When your brain perceives an upcoming event as familiar — even if it's high stakes — it responds with reduced threat response and increased access to established skills.

The routine doesn't calm you down. It signals: *we've been here before. We know what to do.*

## The 7-Day Window

**Day 7 (Last Hard Session)**
This is your final high-intensity training. Go to the well one more time. Don't hold back. A hard Day 7 session reinforces confidence through recent successful execution.

Mental work: After training, sit for 5 minutes. Close your eyes and recall your best recent performance. Feel it. Lock it in as your reference point.

**Day 5 (Volume Cut)**
Cut training volume by 30%, maintain intensity. Your body needs recovery, not more stimulus.

Mental work: Write your competition plan. Not a vague intention — a specific tactical document. The exercise of writing forces clarity.

**Day 3 (The Sharpener)**
Short, sharp session. Perfect technical execution at moderate intensity. No new information.

Mental work: Begin your visualization practice (see Day 1 entry).

**Day 1 (The Prime)**
Neural activation only — fast-twitch movements for 15 minutes. The goal is readiness, not fatigue.

Mental work: Full competition visualization. One complete, uninterrupted mental rehearsal of your performance. Not the outcome — the *process*. Every phase, every decision point, every potential obstacle and your response.

## The 24-Hour Window

This is where most athletes make their biggest mistakes.

**The evening before:**
- Same meal you always eat
- Screen off 90 minutes before sleep
- No opponent research (anxiety trigger)
- No new information of any kind
- Same bedtime as always

The brain treats novelty as a threat signal. Your job the night before is to maintain complete routine. Even if you're too excited to sleep — that's fine. Lying still in a dark room has most of the recovery benefits of sleep.

**The morning of:**
- Wake at your normal time
- Same breakfast
- Brief physical activation (10-15 minute walk or movement sequence)
- No social media
- Limited conversation

## The 60-Minute Window

This is your final mental preparation phase.

**60 minutes out: The Social Firewall**

Your phone goes on Do Not Disturb. Headphones on. You are now inaccessible.

The 60-minute window is compromised by conversation with coaches (already given instruction — you don't need more), well-meaning family members (emotional energy drain), or media (anxiety trigger).

**30 minutes out: Physical Activation**

Dynamic warmup to elevate core temperature and activate fast-twitch fibers. This isn't optional — cold muscles perform worse, and the warmup is also a psychological ritual.

**15 minutes out: The Final Visualization**

One short (3-minute) mental rehearsal. Your start. Your first key moment. Your response to the hardest moment. Your finish.

Don't replay the full performance — you did that on Day 1. Just the critical junctures.

**5 minutes out: The Anchor**

Every elite athlete has a physical or sensory anchor — a stimulus that reliably triggers competitive state. Music, a physical gesture, a breath sequence, a word.

Your anchor should be practiced in training until it works automatically. Used now, it bridges practice state to competition state.

## What Not to Do

- Don't change anything. New gear, new food, new sleep schedule, new warmup: all increase threat response.
- Don't seek reassurance. Asking coaches or teammates to confirm you're ready signals to your brain that you're not.
- Don't over-analyze the opponent. You compete against yourself. Your job is to execute your process.
- Don't try to feel calm. Activation is correct. Reframe it as readiness, not anxiety.

## Build It, Then Lock It

The routine only works through repetition. Use it for every competition — low stakes and high stakes. The neural association between routine and performance state takes dozens of iterations to establish.

By the time the championship arrives, the routine runs itself.
