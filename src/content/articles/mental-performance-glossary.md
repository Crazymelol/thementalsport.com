Sports psychology has its own vocabulary. Understanding these terms doesn't just make you a better reader of the research — it gives you precise language to think about your own mental game.

Here are the 30 most important concepts, defined for working athletes.

---

**Activation / Arousal** — The physiological and psychological state of readiness for performance. Neither too high nor too low is optimal; the optimal zone (the "zone") varies by sport and individual. Activation is not anxiety — it's the energy level that drives performance.

**Attentional Control** — The ability to direct and sustain focus on relevant cues while filtering out irrelevant ones. Trainable through mindfulness practice.

**Attribution** — How athletes explain the causes of their performance outcomes. Athletes who attribute failure to controllable factors (effort, strategy) are more resilient than those who attribute to uncontrollable ones (luck, talent limitations).

**Automaticity** — The execution of a learned skill without conscious attention. Elite motor skills become automatic through extended deliberate practice. Choking is automaticity being disrupted by reactivated conscious attention.

**Choking** — Performance degradation under pressure caused by reinstatement of conscious attention to automated skills. Distinct from underperformance due to inadequate preparation.

**Cognitive Reappraisal** — Changing the emotional impact of a stimulus by changing the way it's interpreted. "This is exciting, not threatening" applied to pre-competition anxiety is cognitive reappraisal.

**Collective Efficacy** — A group's shared belief in its ability to execute the actions required to achieve a goal. A team-level variable that predicts performance independently of individual skill.

**Competition Anxiety** — The sport-specific anxiety response to competitive evaluation. Consists of cognitive anxiety (worry, negative thoughts) and somatic anxiety (physical tension, heart rate elevation).

**Concentration** — The ability to maintain attention on task-relevant information over time. One of the five foundational mental skills for athletes.

**Confidence** — An athlete's belief in their ability to execute the skills required for performance. Based on preparation, past performance, and internal narrative.

**Coping** — Cognitive and behavioral strategies used to manage the demands of competition and training. Problem-focused coping addresses the source of stress; emotion-focused coping manages the emotional response.

**Emotional Regulation** — The ability to influence the type, intensity, and duration of emotional states. Central to performance psychology because emotion directly affects decision-making and motor execution.

**Flow State** — An optimal psychological state characterized by complete absorption in the task, loss of self-consciousness, distorted time perception, and intrinsic reward. Associated with peak performance. Facilitated by appropriate challenge-skill balance.

**Goal Setting** — The systematic process of establishing performance targets. Effective goal setting uses the three-level framework: outcome, performance, and process goals.

**Imagery / Visualization** — Mental rehearsal of performance using vivid, multi-sensory imagination. Activates similar neural pathways to physical practice. Effective imagery uses the PETTLEP framework.

**Internal vs. External Focus** — The direction of attentional orientation: internal focus attends to body mechanics, external focus attends to movement effects and environmental targets. External focus typically produces better performance outcomes.

**Intrinsic Motivation** — Motivation derived from inherent interest in and enjoyment of an activity. More sustainable and more performance-enhancing than extrinsic motivation over long time horizons.

**Kinesiophobia** — Fear of movement or re-injury, common in return-to-sport following injury. Often limits performance beyond what physical capacity warrants.

**Locus of Control** — The degree to which individuals believe they control events in their lives. Internal locus (I influence outcomes) is associated with better athletic development; external locus (luck/others control outcomes) with more fragile performance.

**Mental Toughness** — A multidimensional psychological construct including control, commitment, challenge orientation, and confidence. Trainable through deliberate adversity exposure and psychological skill development.

**Mindfulness** — Non-judgmental present-moment awareness. Applied to sport, mindfulness enhances attentional control, emotional regulation, and recovery from performance errors.

**Optimal Arousal** — The activation level at which an individual performs best. Varies by sport (fine motor sports = lower optimal arousal; explosive sports = higher) and individual.

**Perfectionism** — Setting extremely high standards for performance. Adaptive perfectionism involves high standards with self-compassion for failure; maladaptive perfectionism involves high standards with harsh self-judgment, associated with anxiety and burnout.

**Performance Anxiety** — Anxiety specifically related to evaluation in a performance context. Common, treatable, and distinct from clinical anxiety disorders.

**Pre-Performance Routine** — A standardized behavioral sequence preceding competition that creates psychological consistency, reduces arousal variability, and activates conditioned performance states.

**Psychological Safety** — The team-level belief that it is safe to take risks, speak up, and make mistakes without punishment. Primary predictor of team performance in research.

**Self-Efficacy** — Situation-specific confidence — the belief that you can execute a specific skill in a specific context. Higher self-efficacy consistently predicts better performance.

**Self-Talk** — The internal or external verbal statements athletes make to themselves during training and competition. Instructional and motivational self-talk are performance-enhancing; negative self-talk is performance-degrading.

**Transient Hypofrontality** — The temporary reduction in prefrontal cortex activity during intense physical exercise that facilitates flow state. Explains why overthinking is reduced during maximal physical effort.

**Zone of Optimal Functioning (ZOF)** — Hanin's model of individualized optimal anxiety zones: each athlete has an individual anxiety range in which they perform best. Performance degrades both below and above this range.

---

Understanding these concepts gives you the vocabulary to think precisely about your mental game — to identify specifically what's happening, what's needed, and what to work on.

Precision is the prerequisite for improvement.
