What you say in the car after the game matters more than anything the coach said.

Don't open with the score or the mistakes. Open with five words: "I love watching you play." Then praise one thing they *controlled* — effort, not the result. That's how confidence survives a bad game.

You finally won… so why do you feel empty?

The post-win crash is real: dopamine spikes, then the void. If your identity rides on the *feeling* of winning, you'll chase it and crash every time. Anchor to identity instead — "I compete present." The win becomes evidence, not fuel.

### 3 · short-003.mp4 — Train for pressure first (parents)
Your kid WILL face a penalty, a free throw, a match point. Train it now — not in the moment.

Three steps: 1) exhale longer than you inhale, 2) one physical anchor (same gesture every time), 3) one word — their word. Rehearse till it's boring. Pressure doesn't create skills. It reveals trained ones.

LeBron doesn't calm down under pressure. He slows down. Different thing.

Panic = speed: rushed decisions, rushed breath. Elite athletes don't fight adrenaline, they stretch time — longer exhale, eyes up, one cue word. You breathe your way out first, then you think. Slow is smooth, smooth is fast.

Anxiety and excitement are the same physiology; only the *label* differs. Telling yourself "I'm excited" beats "calm down" — you're steering your body, not fighting it. Nerves don't mean you're not ready. They mean it matters.

### 6 · short-006.mp4 — "You're so talented" backfires (parents)
"You're so talented" is one of the most damaging things you can say to a young athlete.

Talent praise builds a fixed mindset — mistakes start to mean "I'm *not* talented," so they play safe. Swap it: "I'm proud of how you kept pushing." Praise effort, response, courage. That praise survives a losing streak.

Watching the scoreboard mid-game is an attention leak — every thought about your numbers is stolen from the next play. Cue the present: this possession, this rep, this touch. The paradox? The stats show up when you stop chasing them.

Confidence built on applause dies in silence — build it on evidence: your training log, your comebacks, your process wins. After a loss: wait 24 hours, then ask what worked, what didn't, one fix. Then close the book. Mistakes are tuition, not identity.

Big-moment paralysis comes from thinking about the *meaning* of the moment, not the *task*. "This is the final" loads pressure. Shrink the game: not "this is everything" — just "this is one pass." One serve. One stride. Meaning is for after the whistle.

An inner critic mid-game means you're playing one-versus-two. Rewrite it: speak to yourself in second person, by name. "You're trained for this" beats "I'm screwing up" — researchers call it self-distancing, and it measurably lifts performance under stress.

### 12 · short-012.mp4 — Stop the mistake spiral (athletes)
One early mistake. And now your whole game is unraveling.

Errors cascade because you *carry* them. The recovery protocol: 1) a physical reset gesture (wipe your hand on your shorts, same every time), 2) one word — "next," 3) eyes to the next task within three seconds. Mistakes are events, not identities.

### 14 · short-014.mp4 — The 24-hour rule (parents)
Your kid just played terribly. Say nothing about it for 24 hours.

Right after a bad game, emotion is high and learning is closed — anything you say lands as criticism. Wait a day, then ask three questions: what felt good, what was hard, what's one thing to try next time. They talk, you listen. The lesson sticks because *they* found it.

Heart pounding, two minutes before you compete. Do this.

The physiological sigh: double inhale through the nose (one big, one short top-up), then a long slow exhale through the mouth. Three times. It's the fastest evidence-backed way to drop your heart rate — the double inhale reopens collapsed air sacs, the long exhale brakes adrenaline.
