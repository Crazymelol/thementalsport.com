**Caption:**
The neuroscience of choking under pressure.

When stakes are high, the prefrontal cortex — the brain's executive control center — increases monitoring of automated skills.

A tennis serve that has been hit 50,000 times becomes conscious again. The athlete starts "thinking" about a movement that should run on autopilot. Performance degrades.

This is not a character flaw. It is a predictable neurological event.

The solution is not "just relax" or "trust your training." Those are outputs, not inputs.

The inputs are:
→ Pre-competition routines that anchor the nervous system to familiar states
→ Between-point reset protocols that interrupt the monitoring loop
→ Pressure exposure in training that habituates the stress response

Choking is trainable out of an athlete.

But it requires a system — not a pep talk.

### POST 10 — Quick Win Framework
**Visual:** Black bg — 3-step numbered
**Canva text:**
> THE 90-SECOND
> RESET PROTOCOL
> EVERY COACH
> SHOULD TEACH

**Caption:**
When an athlete makes a mistake in competition, the next 90 seconds determines whether it becomes one error or a spiral.

Most athletes have no protocol for this window. Here's the one we install first:

**Step 1 (0-30 seconds): Physical anchor**
Deep breath — double inhale, long exhale. This is physiological, not mental. It directly activates the parasympathetic nervous system and interrupts the cortisol spike.

**Step 2 (30-60 seconds): Cognitive label**
One word or short phrase that signals "that's done." The athlete chooses this in advance. It becomes automatic with repetition. Examples: "next," "process," "reset."

**Step 3 (60-90 seconds): Focus cue**
Return attention to one specific, controllable process element. Not the score. Not the error. One technical cue for the next action.

Practiced in training → automatic in competition.

This is the minimum viable mental performance intervention. Start here.
